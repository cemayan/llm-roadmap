# Career resilience in the LLM era

> Written 2026-07-13. Context: Claude is already deeply integrated into our dev
> processes at work (hooks, skills, agent pipelines). The question: what else
> makes a career durable once that's table stakes?
>
> Core premise: **tool proficiency is not a moat.** In 1-2 years everyone will
> have agent pipelines. Invest in what remains valuable after the tools
> commoditize.

## 1. Depth to audit AI output

As code generation gets cheap, the bottleneck shifts to "is it correct?"
Distributed systems, debugging, performance, security fundamentals don't lose
value — they gain it, because fewer people can spot the one wrong line in an
agent's 2000-line diff.

Practice this actively, not passively:
- Occasional agent-free debugging sessions
- Design first myself, then compare against Claude's take

## 2. From power user to organizational architect

"Engineer who uses Claude well" and "person who designs and **measures** the
team's AI-assisted development process" are different career capital. For the
second:

- Make the integration's impact measurable: review time, defect rate, cycle time
- Turn it into an internal playbook / standard
- Teach it to others

"I built this and it improved X by Y" is the sentence that matters in
promotions and interviews. The existing hooks/skills infrastructure is already
halfway there — **this is the biggest current gap.**

## 3. Portable reputation

Internal reputation stays with the company; it doesn't transfer in a downturn.
The OSS product + posts (Phase 3) are the insurance part of the plan — don't
postpone them.

The LLM + observability combination is the differentiator: generic "AI
engineers" are plentiful; "the person solving observability problems with
LLMs" is rare. Domain depth + LLM skill is much harder to copy than LLM skill
alone.

## 4. Ownership of outcomes, not tasks

The most automation-resistant position is not "does the assigned task" but
"decides which problem is worth solving and owns the result." Product
thinking, talking to users, making trade-off calls. The OSS product is the
training ground — there, I'm also the PM.

## Summary

The roadmap already covers #1, #3, #4. The missing piece is #2: turning the
internal integration work into something visible, measured, and institutional.
Tracked as a Phase 3 item.
